# Sulfuric Acid Shortage and Titanium Dioxide Pigment Thesis

## Overview
China moved to halt ordinary and by-product sulfuric acid exports starting in May 2026, tightening an already stressed global sulfuric acid market.[cite:39][cite:385] That matters for titanium dioxide (TiO2) pigment because the sulfate process remains a major global production route and requires large volumes of sulfuric acid per ton of pigment output.[cite:380][cite:403][cite:409]

## Why the thesis matters
The core idea is that many obvious sulfuric-acid-sensitive sectors such as fertilizer, metals, steel, and broad infrastructure have already been heavily discussed and repriced by the market.[cite:282][cite:283] Titanium dioxide pigment is a more interesting second-order exposure because demand is tied to maintenance and recurring coatings use rather than only to headline capex cycles.[cite:353][cite:354][cite:355]

TiO2 is widely used in highway lane markings, airport runway paints, bridge coatings, and general infrastructure paints because it provides brightness, opacity, and durability.[cite:353][cite:354][cite:357] That makes the demand base relatively sticky even if input costs rise, since agencies and contractors cannot simply stop using visible white markings or durable protective coatings.[cite:353][cite:354]

## Shortage details
China's restriction reportedly covers industrial sulfuric acid exports, including by-product acid from smelting, with electronic-grade material carved out as an exception.[cite:39][cite:385] Several reports also tie the shortage to wider sulfur and logistics stress linked to the Strait of Hormuz, increasing the odds that replacement supply is expensive and unevenly available.[cite:405][cite:390]

Industry commentary and market reporting indicate that sulfuric acid prices have been rising sharply and that TiO2 producers using the sulfate process are facing direct cost pressure and tighter operating conditions.[cite:399][cite:402] Some reports describe weekly TiO2 price increases and production pressure at Chinese plants, reinforcing the view that pigment pricing can move if acid stays constrained.[cite:399][cite:407]

## How sulfuric acid is used in pigment production
There are two main industrial routes for TiO2 production: the sulfate process and the chloride process.[cite:380][cite:381] The sulfate process digests titanium-bearing feedstock such as ilmenite using sulfuric acid, and multiple sources indicate it can require roughly 3 to 4 tons of sulfuric acid per ton of TiO2 output depending on process design and recycling efficiency.[cite:403][cite:409][cite:324]

The chloride process does not rely on sulfuric acid in the same way because it uses chlorine chemistry and typically requires higher-grade feedstock such as rutile or synthetic rutile.[cite:380][cite:381] That makes chloride capacity a partial buffer, but not a full industry solution, because feedstock, plant design, and installed capacity limit rapid switching.[cite:380][cite:381][cite:394]

## Alternatives and limits
There are alternatives, but none are easy or immediate at scale.[cite:380][cite:381]

- **Chloride process:** This is the main industrial alternative to the sulfate process, but it needs different feedstock, different plant configuration, and significant capital, so it is not a quick replacement for sulfate capacity.[cite:380][cite:381]
- **Waste-acid recycling:** TiO2 plants can recover and recycle part of the sulfuric acid stream, which reduces fresh-acid demand, but recycling does not eliminate the need for virgin acid input.[cite:382][cite:395][cite:396]
- **Hydrochloric or nitric acid routes:** Research exists on alternative leaching methods, but these are not established large-scale replacements for mainstream pigment production.[cite:387][cite:394]
- **Using less white pigment:** In most practical applications this is not a real substitute, because TiO2 remains the dominant white pigment for opacity, brightness, and weathering performance in coatings and markings.[cite:353][cite:354][cite:201]

Older or weaker substitutes such as zinc oxide, lithopone, or lead white do not offer the same broad combination of performance, safety profile, and economics for modern large-scale infrastructure coatings.[cite:201][cite:203][cite:198] In other words, the practical alternative is not “stop using white,” but rather “pay more, optimize formulation, recycle more acid, or shift gradually toward chloride-linked supply where possible.”[cite:380][cite:381][cite:395]

## Why this may be less priced in than metals
Steel, aluminum, and broad infrastructure suppliers have been repriced for years around tariffs, Buy America rules, and infrastructure spending.[cite:279][cite:282][cite:283] By contrast, TiO2 pigment is a narrower and less-discussed bottleneck where sulfuric acid scarcity can hit costs more directly without the same level of investor crowding.[cite:399][cite:404]

The market may still underappreciate that this is a maintenance and coatings story, not just a construction-volume story.[cite:353][cite:354] Highway lines, runway markings, bridge coatings, and industrial paints all need recurring replacement and cannot tolerate poor visibility or poor corrosion protection for long.[cite:353][cite:357][cite:362]

## Risks and counterarguments
The biggest counterargument is that chloride-process producers may gain share and soften the impact on end markets.[cite:380][cite:381] Another risk is that acid recycling, formulation changes, or temporary demand weakness in construction and industrial coatings could blunt the margin upside.[cite:382][cite:395]

There is also stock-selection risk: a good industry thesis does not guarantee good equity performance if a company is already near a 52-week high, carries balance-sheet stress, or has company-specific execution problems.[cite:371][cite:373] That is why the thesis is stronger as a framework for cross-examination than as an automatic buy signal.[cite:371][cite:404]

## Working conclusion
The strongest version of the thesis is that sulfuric acid scarcity can tighten the economics of the TiO2 sulfate process just as infrastructure, transportation, and maintenance coatings continue to require high-performance white pigment.[cite:385][cite:399][cite:353] The best public non-OTC names to cross-examine are TROX, CC, and KRO, with particular attention to process mix, feedstock flexibility, balance sheet, and how far each stock already sits from its 52-week high.[cite:404][cite:371][cite:373]
