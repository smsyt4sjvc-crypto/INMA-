# Two-Year Forward Investment Outlook: Nuclear & Fuel Suppliers with Mag 7 and Government Contracts
## Overview
This report evaluates a concentrated basket of nuclear power producers and uranium/fuel suppliers with direct or emerging exposure to Mag 7 hyperscalers (Meta, Amazon, Microsoft, Alphabet, etc.) and/or material contracts with the U.S. government or its agencies.

The tickers analyzed are:

- Constellation Energy (CEG)
- Talen Energy (TLN)
- Eagle Nuclear Energy (NUCL)
- NuScale Power (SMR)
- Cameco (CCJ)
- Vistra (VST)
- BWX Technologies (BWXT)
- Nano Nuclear Energy (NNE)
- Centrus Energy (LEU)
- Uranium Energy Corp (UEC)

The focus is a two-year forward view (mid-2026 through mid-2028) on revenues, earnings power, contract backlogs, valuation, and political support, with specific attention to nuclear’s role in powering AI/data center growth.
## Structural Demand Drivers
### Data Center Power Demand
U.S. data centers consumed roughly 4.4% of total U.S. electricity in 2023 and are projected to reach 6.7%–12% by 2028, implying roughly a doubling to tripling of demand versus 2023 levels. Berkeley Lab estimates data center electricity use rose from about 58 TWh in 2014 to 176 TWh in 2023 and could reach 325–580 TWh by 2028. This growth is heavily driven by AI workloads, hyperscale cloud, and high‑performance computing.[^1][^2][^3]
### Nuclear as Clean Baseload
Small modular reactors (SMRs) and uprated existing reactors provide 24/7, weather‑independent, zero‑carbon power that directly addresses the intermittency limitations of solar and wind for mission‑critical data centers. The U.S. Department of Energy notes that nuclear‑powered data centers can operate with high reliability and minimal emissions, aligning with Big Tech’s 24/7 carbon‑free energy objectives.[^4][^5]
### Public and Political Support
Gallup surveys show U.S. support for using nuclear power rose from 43% in 2020 to about 55% in 2023. A 2025 Pew survey finds roughly six in ten U.S. adults now favor expanding nuclear power plants, up sharply from 43% in 2020. A 2025 global review of nuclear public opinion reports that over 70% of U.S. respondents support nuclear energy, the highest in decades. This backdrop significantly reduces political risk relative to the post‑Fukushima era.[^6][^7][^8]
## Key Mag 7 and Government Contracts
### Constellation Energy (CEG) – Meta / Clinton Plant
Meta signed a 20‑year agreement with Constellation Energy to purchase nuclear power from the Clinton Clean Energy Center in Illinois. Beginning in 2027, Meta will buy essentially the full 1.1 GW output of the plant, plus an incremental 30 MW uprate, under a long‑term power purchase agreement (PPA) designed to keep Clinton operating and support relicensing. While financial terms are undisclosed, the contract anchors long‑duration baseload revenue against a AAA‑quality counterparty (Meta), materially de‑risking the asset.[^9][^10]
### Talen Energy (TLN) – Amazon / Susquehanna Plant
Talen and Amazon signed a long‑term PPA under which Talen will supply 1.9–1.92 GW of nuclear power from the Susquehanna plant in Pennsylvania to AWS data centers through 2042, with options to extend further. The delivery ramps over time and reaches full volume by 2032, providing Talen with a stable revenue stream tied to Amazon’s AI/cloud expansion. Both companies are also exploring co‑located data centers and potential SMR deployments within Talen’s Pennsylvania footprint.[^11][^12]
### Vistra (VST) – Meta / Perry, Davis‑Besse, Beaver Valley
Vistra entered into 20‑year PPAs with Meta to supply 2,609 MW of carbon‑free nuclear capacity from its Perry, Davis‑Besse, and Beaver Valley plants in the PJM region. The company expects partial delivery starting in late 2026 and full delivery by 2027 for existing capacity, with additional uprate capacity phasing in by 2034. Vistra estimates these agreements could increase adjusted free cash flow by roughly 8–10% from operating capacity plus 5–7% from uprate capacity upon full delivery, providing a powerful long‑term FCF growth lever.[^13][^14]
### BWX Technologies (BWXT) – NNSA Defense Fuels
BWXT was awarded a contract valued at approximately $1.5 billion by the U.S. Department of Energy’s National Nuclear Security Administration (NNSA) to develop domestic uranium enrichment capabilities for defense purposes. In addition, BWXT previously secured roughly $2.6 billion in orders for nuclear components for U.S. Navy submarines and an aircraft carrier, reinforcing a large multi‑year government backlog. These contracts provide long‑duration, government‑backed revenue visibility.[^15][^16]
### Centrus Energy (LEU) – DOE HALEU Contract
Centrus has a competitively awarded HALEU Operation Contract with the DOE to produce high‑assay low‑enriched uranium (HALEU) for advanced reactors. In June 2025, Centrus reported it had delivered 900 kg of HALEU UF6 to DOE, fulfilling the Phase 2 production target and entering Option 1a of ongoing production. A June 2025 8‑K disclosed DOE exercised an option to extend production through June 2026 and has additional options for up to eight more years at 900 kg per year, creating a potential multi‑year revenue stream.[^17][^18][^19][^20]
### Uranium Energy (UEC) – U.S. Uranium Reserve
UEC was awarded a contract by DOE/NNSA in 2022 to supply 300,000 pounds of U.S.‑origin uranium concentrates at $59.50 per pound, totaling $17.85 million, for the U.S. strategic uranium reserve, with deliveries completed in early 2023. While this contract is fully executed rather than ongoing backlog, it demonstrates strategic supplier status for U.S. government fuel security initiatives.[^21][^22]
### Nano Nuclear Energy (NNE) – Supermicro MOU
In May 2026, Nano Nuclear signed a strategic, non‑binding memorandum of understanding with Supermicro to explore integrating Nano’s microreactors with Supermicro’s AI server and data center platforms. The collaboration aims to develop on‑site nuclear‑powered data centers and joint go‑to‑market strategies for hyperscale, enterprise, and edge customers, positioning NNE as a speculative but potentially high‑beta play on nuclear‑powered AI infrastructure.[^23][^24]
### NuScale Power (SMR) – SMR Deployment Pipeline
NuScale remains one of the few listed SMR developers with NRC‑certified technology, though its flagship UAMPS project was cancelled. Industry reports indicate NuScale is in discussions with multiple data center operators and secured an agreement with ENTRA1 Energy and the Tennessee Valley Authority for up to 6 GW of SMR deployments, including a proposed 920 MW plant requiring only about 35 acres. However, no public, binding PPAs with specific Mag 7 companies have been disclosed as of mid‑2026.[^25][^26]
### Cameco (CCJ) – Westinghouse and U.S. Government Partnership
Cameco, together with Brookfield and Westinghouse, entered into a strategic partnership with the U.S. government in late 2025 to accelerate deployment of Westinghouse nuclear reactors in the U.S. and globally. The arrangement contemplates at least $80 billion of new reactor investment, with U.S. government support for financing and permitting. While not a direct Mag 7 contract, this positions Cameco as a critical fuel supplier into a large wave of Western nuclear build‑out.[^27][^28]

## Company‑by‑Company Outlook (2026–2028)
### Constellation Energy (CEG)
CEG is the largest U.S. producer of carbon‑free electricity, with a nuclear‑heavy generation fleet and rapidly growing clean‑energy PPA book. The 20‑year Meta‑Clinton deal effectively backstops a 1.1 GW nuclear asset with blue‑chip counterparty risk, supporting long‑term cash flow visibility. Consensus projects mid‑single‑digit revenue growth through 2028 with EPS compounding faster due to operating leverage and capital allocation.[^9][^10][^29][^33]

Valuation is premium, with trailing P/E and EV/EBITDA well above utility peers, but supported by strong earnings growth and a top‑tier clean‑energy profile. Political risk is modest: the portfolio is U.S.‑centric, aligned with decarbonization goals, and directly supports data‑center electrification. Over a two‑year horizon, upside depends on continued execution against PPAs, stable regulatory frameworks, and the pace of additional tech/industrial contracts.[^30][^7][^8]
### Talen Energy (TLN)
TLN’s nuclear‑linked exposure is concentrated in the Susquehanna plant, where the Amazon PPA runs through 2042 and ramps to 1.9 GW by 2032. Consensus estimates show strong revenue and net income growth into 2028, driven by contracted volumes and potential incremental co‑located data‑center developments. TLN is more levered and less diversified than CEG or VST, which increases both upside torque and downside sensitivity to plant performance and plant‑specific regulatory risk.[^11][^12][^31]

On valuation, TLN trades at a discount to CEG/VST on traditional utility metrics but with superior EPS growth projections, reflecting both its smaller scale and perceived risk. Over the next two years, catalysts include AWS ramp schedules, any announcements on SMR development on the Susquehanna site, and potential additional tech PPAs.[^31]
### Vistra (VST)
Vistra has transformed from a Texas‑centric power producer into a national low‑carbon platform anchored by its PJM nuclear assets. The Meta PPAs locking in 2,609 MW over 20 years across Perry, Davis‑Besse, and Beaver Valley are expected to boost adjusted free cash flow by roughly 13–17% once fully ramped, according to company guidance cited in SEC and news reports. Consensus estimates show double‑digit EPS growth and rising free cash flow through 2028, supported by both nuclear and retail/renewables segments.[^13][^14][^32][^40]

Vistra’s valuation is below that of CEG despite similar or faster expected EPS growth and arguably comparable strategic positioning as a nuclear‑anchored clean‑power provider. Political alignment is strong: VST’s projects extend the life of existing plants and add low‑carbon baseload in critical grid regions, matching federal and state decarbonization priorities.[^32][^40][^7][^8]
### BWX Technologies (BWXT)
BWXT is primarily a defense and naval nuclear contractor rather than a power‑plant owner, but it offers one of the cleanest plays on U.S. nuclear re‑industrialization. The $1.5B NNSA enrichment contract plus a $2.6B naval nuclear components order yield a multi‑billion‑dollar backlog extending well beyond two years. Consensus forecasts mid‑single‑digit revenue growth and steady margin expansion through 2028.[^15][^16][^33][^46]

Valuation is rich versus industrial peers given government‑backed cash flows and high technical barriers to entry. Over the next two years, key drivers are execution on NNSA milestones, potential incremental HALEU/advanced‑reactor support awards, and broader defense budget trends. Political risk skew is positive, as both parties emphasize domestic nuclear and defense supply chains.[^41][^19][^20]
### Centrus Energy (LEU)
Centrus is a leveraged play on HALEU supply for advanced reactors, including SMRs and microreactors. The DOE HALEU Operation Contract, with options that could extend production for up to eight additional years, positions LEU as an early domestic producer of fuel critical for next‑gen reactors. Consensus estimates show modest revenues and EPS in 2026–2028, but these may understate upside if HALEU demand inflects as advanced reactors approach commercialization.[^17][^18][^34][^19][^20]

Valuation embeds significant optionality: average price targets of ~247 vs. ~159 current price indicate analysts see material upside but with a mix of buy and neutral ratings reflecting policy and execution risks. Over a two‑year horizon, catalysts include further DOE option exercises, commercial HALEU offtake contracts, and clarity on advanced‑reactor deployment timelines.[^43]
### Cameco (CCJ)
Cameco is one of the largest listed uranium producers, with tier‑one assets and contracts across utilities and nuclear operators worldwide. Its partnership with Westinghouse and Brookfield, combined with U.S. government support for at least $80B of new reactor builds, provides a strong demand backdrop for uranium fuel over the next decade. Consensus ratings skew strongly bullish with high price targets, reflecting expectations of sustained high uranium prices and volume growth.[^27][^42][^46][^28]

Over the next two years, CCJ’s earnings and valuation are primarily leveraged to spot and contract uranium prices rather than individual tech PPAs. Political tailwinds include Western efforts to reduce dependency on Russian and Central Asian uranium supplies.[^21]
### Uranium Energy Corp (UEC)
UEC is a U.S.‑focused uranium company with ISR operations and a large inventory of U.S.‑origin uranium. After supplying 300,000 lbs to the U.S. strategic uranium reserve at $59.50/lb for $17.85M, UEC has positioned itself as a key domestic supplier. Recent financials show a shift from near‑zero revenue to tens of millions in uranium sales and expansionary acquisitions, including Rio Tinto’s Wyoming assets.[^21][^37][^47][^22][^48]

Consensus forecasts steep revenue growth from $23M in FY2026 to around $244M by FY2028, with EBITDA turning strongly positive as production and sales ramp. Analyst ratings are strongly bullish with price targets significantly above the current share price, but execution and commodity risk remain high.[^35]
### NuScale Power (SMR)
NuScale is the only listed SMR developer with an NRC‑approved design but faces project‑specific and financing challenges following the cancellation of its lead U.S. project. Consensus forecasts growing revenues (to roughly $311M by 2028) but negative EBITDA until at least that date, with only marginal EPS profitability projected for 2028. Analyst consensus is Hold with a wide dispersion of price targets, reflecting high uncertainty.[^36][^45][^26]

From an investor perspective, NuScale is a long‑duration option on SMR commercialization; near‑term catalysts include new project announcements, firm offtake contracts, and potential partnerships with data‑center operators.
### Nano Nuclear Energy (NNE)
Nano Nuclear is a pre‑revenue microreactor developer targeting highly modular, transportable reactor designs for defense, remote communities, and potentially data centers. The 2026 MOU with Supermicro provides validation and access to AI/data‑center customers but is non‑binding and early‑stage. Estimates show no meaningful revenue before 2028 and large negative EBITDA and net income.[^23][^24][^37][^49]

NNE is effectively a venture‑style speculative equity with high sensitivity to regulatory events, demonstration milestones, and capital‑raising conditions. Over two years, returns will likely be driven more by newsflow and sentiment than by fundamentals.
### Eagle Nuclear Energy (NUCL)
Public information on Eagle Nuclear Energy is limited; it appears to be a small‑cap nuclear‑themed company with a sub‑$300M market cap and no major disclosed Mag 7 or U.S. government contracts as of mid‑2026. Given the absence of detailed financials and contracts in mainstream data sources, NUCL should be considered high‑risk and speculative.[^46]
## Technical Backdrop (High Level)
Given the strong fundamental tailwinds, many of these names have experienced substantial price appreciation over the past 12–24 months, particularly VST, CEG, CCJ, and UEC. Analyst target prices still indicate upside for most, but technicals show extended trends and elevated volatility, especially in uranium‑linked equities.[^40][^42][^44]

Utilities with contracted nuclear PPAs (CEG, TLN, VST) generally trade in strong uptrends with rising moving averages and higher‑high price structures, reflecting investor preference for contracted clean‑energy cash flows. Uranium producers and developers (CCJ, UEC, LEU) exhibit more cyclical, commodity‑driven price action with sharp corrections around uranium price swings and macro risk‑off episodes.[^42][^44][^40]

SMR/microreactor developers (SMR, NNE) experience even higher beta, with large reactions to news on project cancellations, regulatory milestones, or partnership announcements.[^36][^37]
## Political and Regulatory Environment
The Biden and now Trump administrations have both supported nuclear expansion, albeit via different policy framing—national security, grid reliability, and decarbonization on one hand, and domestic energy dominance and on‑shoring on the other. DOE has launched HALEU and advanced‑reactor support programs, awarding contracts to companies such as Centrus and BWXT to build domestic fuel capabilities. The U.S. has also created a uranium reserve program, under which UEC and others sold material to NNSA.[^17][^21][^16][^18][^22][^19][^20]

Public support for nuclear has risen to multidecade highs, reducing the risk of abrupt policy reversals and enabling state‑level actions to keep existing plants open and fund life‑extension and uprates. However, permitting timelines for new builds remain long, and siting new reactors near data centers, even SMRs, will face local opposition and environmental review, creating timing and cost risks.[^6][^7][^26][^8]

Internationally, Western governments are accelerating nuclear deployment to reduce dependence on Russian energy and meet net‑zero goals, creating a favorable export environment for Cameco, Westinghouse, and associated suppliers.[^27][^28]
## Two‑Year Forward Outlook and Risk Considerations
### Most Direct Mag 7 Leverage
- **High‑Conviction, Contracted Cash Flow Names**: CEG, TLN, and VST stand out for signed, long‑term PPAs directly with Meta and Amazon, with revenue visibility lasting 20+ years. Over a two‑year horizon, these contracts de‑risk earnings and support multiple expansion, provided counterparty and regulatory conditions remain stable.[^9][^11][^13][^10][^12][^14]
- **Government‑Secured Fuel and Defense Plays**: BWXT and LEU provide more indirect leverage to the same theme via government‑backed fuel and defense contracts that underpin advanced reactors and strategic stockpiles.[^17][^16][^18][^19]
- **Commodity Leverage**: CCJ and UEC capture the broader uranium cycle that underlies all new nuclear builds, including data‑center‑driven demand.[^27][^21][^42][^44]
### Key Risks (Cross‑Cutting)
- **Project/Execution Risk**: SMR and microreactor developers such as SMR and NNE face technology, cost, and schedule risk; delays or cancellations can drive sharp equity drawdowns.[^36][^37][^26]
- **Policy and Regulatory Risk**: While macro support is strong, specific projects can still be delayed by local opposition, environmental litigation, or shifting state incentives.[^26]
- **Commodity Price Risk**: Uranium‑focused names (CCJ, UEC, LEU) remain sensitive to spot price corrections and contract renegotiations.[^42][^44]
- **Concentration Risk**: TLN’s dependence on Susquehanna and AWS, or individual plants for CEG/VST, introduces idiosyncratic operational risk despite diversification at the portfolio level.[^11][^13][^10]
### High‑Level Positioning Takeaways
- **Core, lower‑volatility exposure**: CEG and VST as diversified clean‑power platforms with flagship nuclear PPAs.
- **Higher‑beta PPA play**: TLN, which is smaller and more plant‑concentrated but benefits from a transformative Amazon contract.
- **Fuel and defense backbone**: BWXT and LEU as government‑backed infrastructure and fuel suppliers with multi‑year contracts.
- **Uranium cycle leverage**: CCJ and UEC as primary beneficiaries of structurally rising uranium demand.
- **Speculative SMR/microreactor options**: SMR, NNE, and potentially NUCL as long‑duration optionality trades rather than near‑term cash‑flow generators.

Over the 2026–2028 window, the most balanced exposure to the nuclear‑for‑AI theme likely comes from a barbelled approach—anchoring in cash‑flow‑rich utilities (CEG, VST, TLN) and fuel/defense suppliers (BWXT, LEU, CCJ, UEC), while sizing SMR and NNE as small, speculative positions commensurate with their technology and policy risks.
