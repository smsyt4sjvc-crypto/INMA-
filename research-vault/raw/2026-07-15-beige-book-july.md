## The one-line read
**It is now a capital-expenditure-driven economy, not a consumer-driven one** — a Philadelphia
manufacturing contact said exactly that, and the whole book bears it out: data-center / AI / defense
capex is the load-bearing beam holding up manufacturing, construction, energy demand and skilled-labor
hiring, while the consumer (especially the bottom half) frays. Prices moderated but did NOT accelerate;
the margin squeeze (input costs > selling prices) is real and holding — firms eating it, so far.

---

## DATA — Overall activity (National Summary)
- Activity up **slight-to-moderate in 11 of 12 Districts**, 1 flat (prior: 10 up, 1 flat, 1 down). Firmer.
- Manufacturing production grew modestly-to-moderately in most Districts, **"led by stronger orders
  from the data center, machinery, and defense sectors."**
- Construction/RE up slightly, **"with several Districts noting growth in data center building."**
- Consumer spending only **edged up**; higher prices (esp. fuel) dampened other categories; several
  Districts noted trading down / discretionary declines.
- Financial conditions stable; commercial + consumer loan volumes up modestly. **"Commercial loan
  quality was stable, but consumer loan quality ticked down."**
- Ag deteriorated (low commodity prices, high input costs, tighter credit). Oil & gas drilling up.
- Tourism up (World Cup boost). Autos: flat sales, repair spend up (holding vehicles longer).

## DATA — Prices (the margin-vs-pass-through tell)
- **Prices up moderately: 9 Districts moderate, 2 robust (Cleveland, St. Louis), 1 slight. Vs prior
  period, price growth was "the same or slower in ALL Districts."** → NOT accelerating.
- **"A couple of Districts reported that selling prices grew less than input costs over the period,
  crimping margins."** Pass-through explicitly MIXED and firm-by-firm.
- **Richmond hard numbers:** service-sector price growth **dipped below 3.5% YoY**; service non-labor
  input costs **above 5.5%**; manufacturing non-labor input costs **just below 7%** — but **"prices
  received by manufacturers remained relatively the same."** → Input 5.5–7% vs output <3.5%. Squeeze
  quantified. Manufacturers eating ~3+ points.
- **Kansas City:** "Inflationary pressures continued to compress profit margins, prompting firms to
  make pricing and investment adjustments" — selective price hikes + cost-saving tech, watching
  consumer responsiveness.
- **Cleveland (robust):** "Some contacts reported passing along all the cost increases… some passed on
  only part… and some did not pass along any… for fear of losing customers." The pricing-power map, live.
- **NY:** input prices rising strongly; selling at high end of moderate. **"an upstate NY technical
  consulting firm reported sharp price increases for memory and storage components driven by elevated
  demand from data center buildouts."** ← MEMORY INFLATION in the Fed's own survey.
- **St. Louis (robust, widespread):** electricity provider — "supply-chain shortages have sharply
  increased the cost of energy-infrastructure materials." Vendors "adding inflation-indexed price
  adjustments to service contracts, previously uncommon." Agribusiness: fertilizer cost pressure
  "expected to persist through 2027."
- **Atlanta:** "the accelerated build-out of the infrastructure for emerging AI technologies contributed
  to supply constraints and higher prices for steel, transformers, and other machinery."
- Dominant cost driver named across nearly every District = **the conflict in the Middle East** (fuel,
  freight, petrochemicals, fertilizer) + tariffs.

## DATA — the AI / data-center capex signature (everywhere)
- **Philadelphia (THE quote):** manufacturers "reported continued strength in activities related to
  data centers, AI, and defense-related manufacturing. This strength led one contact to say that they
  believe **it is now a capital expenditure-driven economy rather than a consumer-driven one.**"
  Also: "a shortage of electricians has delayed data center projects in the region." Nonresidential
  construction "continued… by firms in the data center and related manufacturing spaces but little new
  activity elsewhere."
- **Minneapolis (concentration in GDP terms):** *"If you are not directly involved in data center work,
  the broader construction market feels relatively weak,"* — electrical contractor, North Dakota.
- **Dallas (the power ceiling + pushback):** "data center and industrial construction were robust. Data
  center capacity demand has increased, but **new projects are being hindered by limited access to
  energy, supply chain disruptions, and public pushback.**" Oilfield services "noted increased
  competition for skilled labor from the power generation and AI industries." Energy contacts flagged
  "mounting challenges beyond the 2026 budget cycle regarding electric power supply and water disposal."
- **Cleveland:** "Data center development continued to drive demand for metal products and electrical
  components." RE robust in "data center and electricity generation segments."
- **Chicago:** nonresidential construction "remained robust in the data center and electricity
  generation segments"; machinery sales up strongly "including to defense and data center builders";
  "shortages of inputs, notably for electrical components."
- **Atlanta:** "Industrial energy demand remained strong, largely because of accelerating investments
  in AI-related infrastructure across the Southeast"; data-center-construction hiring; transformer supply.
- **NY:** "Commercial real estate markets remained strong… Demand was robust among AI-related firms
  seeking space." ← Jake's AI-office call (office-for-employees), in a Fed survey.
- **SF:** employers "continuing to invest in productivity-enhancing AI technologies."
- **Boston, Cleveland, Atlanta, KC, SF:** AI showing up as a LABOR force — white-collar layoffs from AI
  efficiency (Boston), AI application screeners blocking jobseekers (KC), broad productivity deployments.

## DATA — the consumer (K-shape / bottom-half stress)
- Bifurcation near-universal: high-income resilient (Atlanta notes "emerging signs of softening" even
  there); low/middle trading down, buying less/more often, cutting discretionary.
- **Dallas:** "One nonprofit reported that demand for food assistance has **surpassed levels experienced
  during the financial crisis and the pandemic.**"
- **St. Louis:** social-service nonprofit — "meal demand rose about **30 percent over the past two
  months,** reflecting declining consumer purchasing power and reductions in SNAP benefits."
- **Kansas City:** "roughly **half of the new auto loans** they originate involve borrowers with
  **negative equity** from a trade-in." Bankers note "auto loan stress is increasing… not deteriorated
  to crisis level." Home-furnishings firm: "persistent inflation is permanently shifting middle-income
  households toward lower-cost imported goods."
- **Atlanta:** rise in "buy now, pay later" financing "pointed to ongoing household financial strain."
- **Boston:** "Lower-income consumers reportedly added credit card debt to pay for essential goods."
- **SF:** "faster spending of tax refunds and rising credit card balances."
- Fuel/gas prices repeatedly named as the thing hammering commuters and low-income budgets.

## DATA — labor
- Employment rose on balance (5 Districts modest/moderate/solid gains vs only 1 prior). Low-hire/low-fire.
- **Skilled-worker shortage** named repeatedly — electricians, technicians, tradespeople, machinists,
  welders — much of it tied to the data-center/AI/defense build.
- Wage growth modest-to-moderate, **softening at the margin:** Minneapolis — "We find ourselves no
  longer in a bidding war for talent"; "runaway starting incentives have largely flattened."
- Dallas is the hot spot: "Employment growth was solid… June was their best month since before the
  pandemic"; firms expect 3.5% wage growth next 12mo (up from 3.1% in March).

## DATA — credit / financing fragility
- National: **consumer loan quality ticked down** while commercial stable — stress in the WEAKEST
  borrower first.
- **St. Louis:** "loans to households with weaker credit continued to deteriorate, reflecting growing
  stress among borrowers without strong balance sheets"; small-dollar loan activity to "highly
  cash-constrained customers" up.
- Delinquency upticks: residential mortgages (NY), consumer mortgages (Richmond), lower-income + small
  biz (St. Louis), auto (KC).
- **Kansas City CRE (the equity-injection tell):** "most transactions involved owners contributing
  additional capital or accepting a smaller ownership share"; credit for new development "modestly
  harder to access, particularly multifamily"; floating-rate usage "stalled." Modest CRE loan-quality
  deterioration.
- **SF:** "increased level of substandard loans"; emerging small-business concerns.
- Business/corporate credit largely STABLE — the crack is consumer + CRE, not yet corporate.
- **Capex-expensing policy pull-forward:** KC + Dallas — firms pursuing "facility expansions and
  relocations to take advantage of enhanced investment expensing provisions." Policy-driven capex.

## DATA — war / energy timeline tension (important)
- The survey window (through **July 6**) caught a **DE-ESCALATION**: **Dallas — "the agreement to
  reopen the Strait of Hormuz, which has caused gasoline prices to fall"**; Chicago ag — "fertilizer and
  fuel purchases dropped as transportation blockages eased in the Middle East"; Cleveland/Richmond builders
  expecting relief "pending a resolution of the conflict."
- ⚠️ **This POST-DATES into a reversal:** Jake's live feeds (7/12–7/13, logged in [[demand-destruction]]:
  `raw/2026-07-12-sunday-hormuz-declared-closed.md`, `raw/2026-07-13-zh-trump-blockade-20pct-toll.md`,
  US 140-strikes) show the truce BROKE after July 6 — Trump reinstated the blockade, Brent back to ~$85.
  So the Beige Book's "falling fuel / easing" optimism is a **snapshot of a truce that has since
  reversed** — read its price-relief expectations as stale by ~a week.

## THESIS (interpretation — NOT fact)
- *(analysis + Jake's crux)* The Philly "capex-driven not consumer-driven economy" line is the single
  most important sentence for [[consumption-vs-investment-crux]]: the post-COVID/AI borrowing is
  **BUILDING** (real capex — data centers, machinery, defense), and the weakness is on the **CONSUMER**
  side, exactly the two-axis split (build-aggregate + bear-distribution). BUT the fragility caveat holds:
  that capex is debt/equity-financed and FCF-negative at the hyperscaler level
  ([[ai-financing-fragility]]) — building, on financing that may not pencil.
- *(analysis)* **The margin-vs-price tell resolved: "margins, not prices — so far."** Input costs
  5.5–7% vs selling <3.5% (Richmond), pass-through mixed, price growth same-or-slower everywhere. Warsh's
  "persistently elevated" is NOT yet showing as accelerating consumer pass-through in the Fed's own
  survey — it's showing as a MARGIN squeeze. Pricing power decides who eats it; the bottom-half consumer
  is too price-sensitive to pass to. Watch: if firms stop eating it and start passing → CPI channel opens.
- *(analysis)* **Concentration is now visible in GDP, not just the S&P.** Minneapolis's "if you're not
  in data center work, construction feels weak" is [[concentration]] / [[market-fragility]] narrow-market
  thesis rendered in the REAL economy. The same handful of AI names holding up the index are holding up
  physical construction and manufacturing orders. Narrow breadth is a STATE (per WARNING-vs-TRIGGER
  rule) — this doesn't time anything, but it quantifies the dependency.
- *(analysis)* **Memory inflation confirmed at ground level** (NY: "sharp price increases for memory and
  storage components driven by data center buildouts") — supports the near-term [[ai-capex-cycle]] /
  MU-DRAM shortage read; the shortage is real enough to show up in a Fed contact survey.
- *(analysis)* **Power/permitting ceiling confirmed** (Dallas "limited access to energy… public
  pushback"; Philly electrician shortage delaying data centers; Atlanta transformer constraint) — this is
  [[power-not-petroleum]] / [[buildout-bottleneck-map]] in the Fed's words. Public pushback + power short
  = the physical governor on the capex boom.
- *(analysis)* **Credit stress is consumer + CRE first, corporate later** — matches the vault's "crack
  shows in the weakest borrower first" frame. The KC CRE equity-injection detail (owners putting in more
  capital / taking smaller stakes to close) is a quiet refinancing-stress tell.
