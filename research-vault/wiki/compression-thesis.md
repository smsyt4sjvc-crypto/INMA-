Predicted **"choppy horizontal compression years to fund this thing"** — a sideways/range digestion,

## Why 2000 is the WRONG analogy; the Nifty Fifty is the RIGHT one (Jake's catch)
- **2000 = promise financed by speculation** (no-earnings dotcoms, eyeball metrics). Capital vanished
  because there were no cash flows underneath. **"In 2000 the internet didn't already own the world."**
- **2026 = incumbency financed by cash flow.** The buildout is funded by the most profitable cash machines
  in history. Even under-monetized for years, they don't go to zero — search/ads/cloud/GPUs keep throwing
  off cash (the bear conceded this). Downside is **bounded**: compression, not extinction.
- **The right precedent = Nifty Fifty (1972):** dominant, profitable, world-owning blue chips (Coke, IBM,
  Disney, McDonald's) that were nonetheless **overpriced** → a **lost decade** where *earnings grew into
  the valuation instead of the valuation growing.* Companies never failed; the *price* was too high. That
  is "choppy horizontal compression," historically instantiated.
- ⚠️ Guardrail: the Nifty-Fifty compression came **with** the '73–74 drawdown (−45%). "World-owners bound
  the *ultimate* downside" ≠ "they skip the 25–35% drop *en route*." Compression and a sharp transition
  drawdown are **not** mutually exclusive — the road dips before it digests. (This is why powder exists.)

## The resolution mechanism (Jake — uses the bear's own key variable as the hinge)
The compression is the GAP between costs racing ahead now and healing later. It closes when:
1. **Supply catches up with demand** — memory glut resolves the DRAM/HBM shortage; power eases (turbines
   now, nuclear/SMR behind) → the input costs of each new capex dollar fall.
2. **Capex growth stops accelerating** (land-grab phase ends) → the **growth RATE of depreciation slows**
   (the bear's "never considered" variable, turned into the healing mechanism).
3. **Revenue grows into the accumulated capex** (monetization arrives) → FCF inflects positive.
→ Valuations heal **by earnings rising to price**, not price falling to earnings. A *time* correction.

## The razor's edge (where the whole thesis lives or dies)
**The SAME event — capex growth decelerating / depreciation growth slowing — is BOTH the bull's healing
AND the bear's trigger.** Which one it is is decided by **SEQUENCING:**
- Revenue catches up **before** capex rolls over → depreciation slows into rising revenue → **HEAL** (bull).
- Capex rolls over **before** revenue arrives → it rolled over *because demand disappointed* → depreciation
  grinds on a base that isn't earning → **COMPRESS on disappointment** (bear).
Same mechanical event, opposite outcome. The crux is no longer "does productivity come" — it's **"does it
come before or after the capex rolls over."** Watch the sequencing (the [[how-to-get-paid]] Feb-2027
per-share window is the read: capex decel + depreciation accel + FCF — is revenue absorbing it yet?).

## Positioning implication (closes the bottleneck≠hedge thread for good)
"Supply catches up" **heals the input-PAYERS and kills the input-SELLERS.** The memory glut that relieves
Google's cost line is terminal for the memory maker's pricing power; the power that eases Amazon's bill
ends the merchant generator's scarcity rent. So **if you believe your own resolution thesis (supply
normalizes), it argues for owning the discounted input-payers (the diversified hyperscalers taking lumps
now) and FADING the scarcity-sellers priced at 40–80x today** ([[power-scarcity-equities]] correction).
The opposite of where the crowd is standing. The resolution mechanism doesn't just say the market heals —
it names which side to be on when it does.

## The scope on "they'll be fine"
The **companies** will be fine — diversified incumbents heal. Whether the **shareholder** is fine still
rides entry price + sequencing. "Coke will be fine" and "Coke-at-1972 was a lost decade" were both true.
The denominator survives every revolution ([[how-to-get-paid]]: old economy retired, denominator eternal).
